## Series Overview

Greenhouse gas accounting begins with individual processes — a coal plant burning carbon, a forest absorbing CO₂, a rice paddy releasing methane — and builds toward the full global budget. This series develops the quantitative foundation for understanding how carbon and methane move through the Earth system, using stoichiometry and mass balance rather than vague assertions. Every flux is derived, every emission factor justified from first principles.

## Pedagogical Philosophy

**One process at a time.** Begin with single fluxes: combustion chemistry, photosynthesis, air-sea exchange. Understand each mechanism before combining into budgets.

**Stoichiometry and mass balance.** Every calculation starts with balanced chemical equations and conservation of mass. Units tracked rigorously.

**Derive, don't assert.** Emission factors, uptake rates, atmospheric lifetimes — all derived from fundamental chemistry and physics, not looked up in tables (though we validate against published values).

**Hand calculations first.** Work through carbon balance for a forest stand, a coal plant, a hectare of rice paddy by hand before scaling to global budgets.

**Build complexity incrementally.** Single flux → process pair → sector budget → regional budget → global budget.

## Learning Objectives

1. **Calculate combustion emissions** from fuel carbon content and stoichiometry
2. **Quantify carbon stock changes** from land use conversion
3. **Estimate photosynthetic uptake** from light-use efficiency
4. **Model air-sea CO₂ exchange** via Henry's Law and gas transfer velocity
5. **Calculate soil carbon turnover** from first-order kinetics
6. **Understand methanogenesis** in anaerobic environments
7. **Compute methane atmospheric lifetime** from OH sink
8. **Build farm-scale GHG inventories** using IPCC Tier 1 methods
9. **Close a complete carbon budget** and quantify residual uncertainties
10. **Calculate agricultural N₂O emissions** and evaluate mitigation options

## Model Sequence

### Cluster AM: Carbon Sources (Models 115–117)

**Model 115: Fossil Fuel Combustion Chemistry**
Combustion stoichiometry. Carbon content of fuels. Emission factors from first principles. Calculate CO₂ from fuel consumption. Incomplete combustion.

**Model 116: Biomass Burning and Pyrogenic Carbon**
Combustion completeness. Modified combustion efficiency. Pyrogenic carbon partition: CO₂, CO, CH₄, char. Prescribed burn emissions. Fire return interval and carbon neutrality.

**Model 117: Land Use Change and Deforestation**
Carbon stocks: aboveground, belowground, soil, litter. Stock-change method. Conversion emissions and timescales. IPCC Tier 1/2/3 methods.

### Cluster AN: Carbon Sinks (Models 118–120)

**Model 118: Photosynthesis and Gross Primary Production**
Photosynthesis equation. Light-use efficiency. GPP vs NPP vs NEP. Carbon use efficiency. Seasonal variation and species differences.

**Model 119: Ocean Carbon Uptake and Air-Sea Exchange**
Henry's Law. Solubility pump. Biological pump. Gas exchange velocity. Temperature effects on uptake capacity.

**Model 120: Soil Carbon Storage and Decomposition**
Inputs, outputs, residence time. Temperature sensitivity (Q₁₀). Moisture effects. Steady-state analysis. Agricultural carbon sequestration potential.

### Cluster AO: Methane Processes (Models 121–123)

**Model 121: Methanogenesis in Anaerobic Environments**
Anaerobic decomposition pathway. Substrates and redox conditions. CH₄:CO₂ ratio. Wetland and rice paddy emissions. Environmental controls.

**Model 122: Methane Oxidation and Atmospheric Removal**
Aerobic methanotrophy. Tropospheric OH oxidation. Atmospheric lifetime calculation. OH feedback: CH₄ increase → longer lifetime → more accumulation.

**Model 123: Agricultural Methane — Rice and Livestock**
Enteric fermentation. Emission factors per animal. Manure management. Rice paddy water management as mitigation lever. Livestock feed additives.

### Cluster AP: Integrating Budgets (Models 124–126)

**Model 124: Constructing a Complete Carbon Budget**
Budget equation: Sources − Sinks = Atmospheric Change. Fossil fuels, land use change, ocean sink, land sink (residual). Global Carbon Project data. Uncertainty propagation.

**Model 125: The Global Methane Budget**
Natural and anthropogenic sources. OH sink and soil uptake. Lifetime and burden relationship. Isotopic constraints for source attribution. Post-2007 acceleration.

**Model 126: Nitrous Oxide and the Nitrogen Cascade**
N cycle: fixation → nitrification → denitrification → N₂O. Fertilizer emission factors. GWP and atmospheric lifetime (110 years). Mitigation: precision application, nitrification inhibitors.

## Key Insights

1. **Mass balance reveals gaps.** The terrestrial sink is a residual — high uncertainty signals what we don't yet understand.

2. **Fossil vs biomass emissions differ fundamentally.** Fossil adds new carbon; biomass burning recycles active carbon — but timing of regrowth matters.

3. **Methane lifetime is a feedback.** Rising CH₄ consumes OH, extending its own lifetime — a positive feedback.

4. **Agricultural CH₄ is manageable.** Water management and feed additives can cut emissions 30–80%.

5. **N₂O is persistently dangerous.** 110-year atmospheric lifetime; every kilogram stays problematic for generations.

6. **Uncertainty is quantifiable.** E_FF ±5%, E_LUC ±50%, S_LAND ±100%. Knowing uncertainty structure matters as much as the central estimate.
